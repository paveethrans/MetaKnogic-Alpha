From millions of papers to a queryable metabolism–cancer Knowledge HyperGraph

The Onco-Metabolite Knowledge HyperGraph (GKHG) is built to map the metabolic landscape of cancer with depth and clarity. It continuously ingests millions of full-text biomedical articles, evaluates them using a quantitative relevance model, and extracts complex, multi-entity relationships through Knowledge-Graph RAG. The result isn't just data. It’s a living, queryable knowledge hypergraph where metabolites, pathways, genes, interventions and cancer phenotypes are explicitly connected. Every relationship is traceable to the original source and ranked by strength, context and biological relevance. Researchers can explore metabolism-cancer mechanisms at scale, test hypotheses, surface hidden links and accelerate discovery in real time.

What problem it solves:

Biomedical knowledge is scattered across millions of unstructured articles. Critical relationships - like how a nutrient shapes a metabolic pathway that drives tumor behavior - are buried in prose and rarely modeled in a way that can be queried or recombined.

Most AI tools can summarize research, but they lose the logic behind the connections. You get answers, but not understanding. GKHG is built to fix that. It turns scientific literature into a structured, explainable knowledge graph where relationships between genes, drugs, diseases, mechanisms and outcomes stay explicit, traceable and reusable.
Researchers can ask precise questions, explore pathways, validate mechanisms and generate new hypotheses with transparency. Every insight is backed by sources, so you can see not just what the model says, but why.
GKHG enables literature computable and actionable. It turns buried knowledge into decision-ready insight for translational oncology and beyond.

What is the underlying system:

At its core, GKHG is a generalized knowledge hypergraph for metabolism and cancer. Biological entities - genes, metabolites, nutrients, pathways, diseases, drugs and signaling processes - are modeled as nodes.
Evidence sentences from the literature become hyperedges that connect multiple entities at once (for example, a metabolite, a gene, a pathway and a cancer subtype in a single mechanistic statement).

Each hyperedge keeps its original text, metadata and vector representation. This allows the system to support both graph-structured reasoning and traditional embedding-based retrieval, while always anchoring answers in concrete experimental evidence.

HyperGraph workflow

  • 1
    Ingest
    Pull full-text XML/HTML from PMC and metadata from OpenAlex. Align papers across the two sources to obtain both content and citation context for each article.
  • 2
    Filter and refine
    Apply hard and fuzzy matching using a metabolism–cancer term list. Compute relevance scores (see right) and drop low-quality or off-topic papers.
  • 3
    Extract (Knowledge-Graph RAG)
    For each chunk, Knowledge-Graph RAG extracts entities (metabolites, cancers, pathways, conditions), hyperedges (multi-entity statements), natural-language descriptions and vector embeddings.
    Each hyperedge is one evidence sentence connecting multiple entities.
  • 4
    Build GKHG
    Construct a bipartite graph with entity nodes on one side and hyperedge nodes on the other. Store text, metadata and embeddings for both, enabling graph search and vector retrieval.
  • 5
    Query and reasoning
    Embed the user query, retrieve nearest entities, follow their incident hyperedges and aggregate the most relevant evidence sets before passing them to the language model.
  • 6
    Answer construction and validation
    The language model synthesizes an answer grounded in the retrieved entities and hyperedges. Responses can then be inspected, re-weighted by relevance and subjected to biological sanity checks.

Relevance scoring at scale

Every paper receives a continuous relevance score that combines text match, citation impact, recency and journal prestige. Each component is mapped to a 0–1 quantile rank, making the score robust to extreme outliers.

Text signal (35%)

Section-aware boosting and local keyword density:

\[ T_{1} = \mathrm{qrank}_{01}(\text{section boost}), \quad T_{2} = \mathrm{qrank}_{01}(\text{density per 1000 words}) \] \[ \text{text signal} = 0.8\,T_{1} + 0.2\,T_{2} \]

Citation signal (35%)

Log-transform citation counts before ranking:

\[ C = \log\bigl(1 + \text{citation count}\bigr) \] \[ \text{citation signal} = \mathrm{qrank}_{01}(C) \]

Recency signal (15%)

Down-weight very old work while clipping extremes:

\[ R = \mathrm{clip}\bigl(\text{publication year} - \text{baseline year}, -10, 10\bigr) \] \[ \text{recency signal} = \mathrm{qrank}_{01}(R) \]

Journal prestige signal (15%)

Blend multiple journal-level indicators:

\[ J = 0.55\,\mathrm{qrank}_{01}(\text{SJR index}) + 0.20\,\mathrm{qrank}_{01}(\text{cites per document (2y)}) + 0.15\,\mathrm{qrank}_{01}(\text{citescore}) + 0.10\,\mathrm{qrank}_{01}(\text{journal h-index}) \] \[ \text{journal prestige signal} = \mathrm{qrank}_{01}(J) \]

Final relevance score

\[ \text{final score} = 0.35\,\text{text signal} + 0.35\,\text{citation signal} + 0.15\,\text{recency signal} + 0.15\,\text{journal prestige signal} \]

Here, \(\mathrm{qrank}_{01}(\cdot)\) maps each metric to its empirical quantile rank in \([0,1]\), so different signals become comparable and resistant to a few extremely cited or extremely recent papers.

Why KG + RAG instead of GPT-only?

Large biomedical language models are excellent readers, but they don’t maintain a stable, queryable memory of millions of mechanistic claims. The hypergraph acts as that structured memory layer.

LLM-only (GPT-style)
  • Strong local synthesis, but no explicit global graph.
  • Can hallucinate multi-step mechanisms and interactions.
  • Hard to see which studies and pathways are actually driving an answer.
  • No direct way to trace metabolite → gene → pathway → condition chains.
Knowledge-Graph RAG + GPT (ours)
  • Entities and hyperedges come from explicit sentences in specific papers.
  • Multi-way relations (drug, gene, pathway, nutrient, condition) are preserved as hyperedges.
  • Every answer can be traced back to concrete evidence and literature sources.
  • Graph structure enforces factual grounding and makes the reasoning path inspectable.
