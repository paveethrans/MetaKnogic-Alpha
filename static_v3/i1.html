About this project

This demo sits on top of a large-scale biomedical knowledge graph and a HypergraphRAG pipeline designed for metabolite–cancer reasoning.

Data foundation

We start from large, public full-text and metadata sources:

  • PMC full text: ~7 million papers (excluding manuscripts).
  • OpenAlex: ~40 million papers with citation metadata.
  • Alignment: PMC ↔ OpenAlex matching yields ~6.6 million linked papers.
  • Domain filtering: ~3,400 metabolite, cancer, and pathway keywords (including synonyms) are used to filter to ~5.5 million highly relevant papers.

This gives us a broad but focused corpus for metabolite–cancer biology and related pathways.

Relevance scoring and paper selection

Each candidate paper is scored using multiple signals. All raw components are converted to quantile ranks qrank01(·) to make the score robust to heavy tails and outliers.

  • Text signal (35%) – how strongly the paper’s content matches the domain:
    T1 = qrank01(section_boost), T2 = qrank01(density_per_1k)
    text_signal = 0.8·T1 + 0.2·T2
  • Citation signal (35%) – how impactful the work is:
    C = log(1 + cited_by_count)
    cit_signal = qrank01(C)
  • Recency signal (15%) – gentle preference for newer work:
    Rsig = (pub_year − base_year, −10, 10)
    recency_signal = qrank01(Rsig)
  • Journal prestige (15%) – combines several indexing metrics:
    J = 0.55·qrank01(sjr_idx) + 0.20·qrank01(cites_doc_2y) + 0.15·qrank01(citescore) + 0.10·qrank01(hindex)
    journal_signal = qrank01(J)

All of these are combined into a single relevance score:

relevance = 0.35·text_signal + 0.35·cit_signal + 0.15·recency_signal + 0.15·journal_signal

HypergraphRAG workflow

The pipeline uses HypergraphRAG to extract structured, multi-entity relations from the filtered corpus and to support graph-aware question answering.

  • Ingest: Full-text from PMC + citation graph from OpenAlex.
  • Filter & refine: Hard and fuzzy keyword search (e.g., “R-Glucose” and synonyms), citation-weighting, and removal of low-signal papers.
  • Extract: HypergraphRAG calls an LLM to extract:
    • Entities (metabolites, cancers, pathways, conditions, nutrients…)
    • Hyperedges (multi-way relations with rich context sentences)
    • Textual descriptions and embedding vectors.
  • Build & store: All entities, hyperedges, and their embeddings are stored as a Global Knowledge Hypergraph (GKHG).

Query → graph → answer

When you type a question in the demo, the backend runs a structured workflow:

  • 1. Encode query: Convert the question into an embedding.
  • 2. Entity search: Retrieve nearest entities from the entity vector index.
  • 3. Graph expansion: Walk the hypergraph to find connected hyperedges and co-participating entities.
  • 4. Evidence retrieval: Pull the original supporting snippets from the underlying papers.
  • 5. Answer construction: Feed entities, hyperedges, and snippets into an LLM, which composes a natural-language answer grounded in the graph.
  • 6. Validation (offline): Human review of key edges and answers, and weighting by relevance when needed.

Why a knowledge graph + RAG, not just GPT?

  • Factual grounding: GPT-style models can hallucinate; the graph keeps answers anchored to explicit, inspectable evidence.
  • Multi-entity biology: Real biology involves drug–gene–pathway–condition–nutrient interactions. Hyperedges capture these multi-way relationships directly; binary edges do not.
  • Scale: Millions of papers and relations are stored structurally rather than “memorized” in model weights.
  • Explainability: For each answer, you can see which entities and evidence nodes were involved, and how they connect in the graph.

The demo you see is just the front door into this pipeline: a graph-aware reader over a carefully filtered, scored, and structured biomedical corpus.
